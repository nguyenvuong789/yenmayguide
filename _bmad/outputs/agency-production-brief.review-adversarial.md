## Original Findings And Resolutions

The findings below are kept as an audit trail. They have already been patched into `_bmad/outputs/agency-production-brief.md`; see the resolution summary above for where each fix landed.

1. The brief assumes the agency can interpret "quiet, warm, tactile, cinematic" correctly. That is subjective. Without visual references or examples, they can still deliver generic beige fashion content.

2. The brief gives a 4-hour shoot plan but no contingency if the alley is noisy, rainy, too dark, or visually messy. V1 and V5 depend heavily on environment quality.

3. "First 5 strongest videos" is a goal, not an operating standard. The brief needs explicit pass/fail criteria for each first cut.

4. The agency is told not to show the owner publicly, but the brief still says owner-invisible can include silhouette/back-shot. That can still reveal too much if not controlled.

5. V2 asks for 8-10 rejected pieces. If the shop cannot prepare enough clear rejects, the agency may stage weak examples or exaggerate flaws.

6. V3 requires a hero product but does not define how to choose one. Without criteria, the agency may pick the most visually loud piece instead of the most strategically useful one.

7. V4 "Early Y2K vs late Y2K" risks making an unverified era claim. The brief says not to claim exact era/year without evidence, but V4 invites era framing. This needs a safer fallback.

8. The brief says use keywords in overlays, captions, hashtags, and voiceover when possible, but does not define who writes captions or who checks keywords before posting.

9. Facebook product posts say `DM để giữ hàng`, but Facebook page CTA is `Get Directions`. That is fine strategically, but the agency may confuse page-level CTA with post-level CTA unless explicitly separated.

10. Google Maps photo output is included, but there are no rules for which photos not to upload. Bad Maps photos can damage trust more than no photos.

11. The brief includes product availability confirmation before posting, but not a live inventory owner. If a product is sold before a post goes live, the content can create frustration.

12. No handoff format is specified. If agency sends assets through chat apps with random filenames, the shop will lose track of which video maps to which caption, product, and channel.

13. There is no revision limit or timing. If first cuts arrive late or need large edits, launch timing breaks.

14. The brief includes multiple platforms but no priority if time runs short. Agency needs to know TikTok/IG Reels first, Facebook second, Google Maps photos third, Threads last.

15. The approval checklist is good, but there is no named approver or approval deadline. A checklist without an owner becomes optional.

16. No explicit instruction says to remove TikTok watermark before Facebook/Instagram cross-post. It says do not share TikTok watermark links, but exports also need to be clean.

17. "Natural ambient sound" may be unusable in a busy alley. The brief should require backup audio or clean separate sound capture.

18. The brief does not specify video cover frames. Bad cover frames can reduce click-through even if the video is good.

19. The brief does not define how to handle sold products after content is already produced. If a hero product sells before posting, the agency needs an alternative caption or replacement product.

20. It does not include a one-page agency summary. A 600+ line brief is useful internally, but the agency may not read it fully. It needs an executive one-pager at the top.

## Highest-Risk Failure Modes

1. Agency makes visually pretty but strategically generic Reels.
2. Product info is wrong or stale when posted.
3. Owner privacy is accidentally compromised.
4. V4 makes unverified era claims.
5. Cross-posting creates messy duplicate assets with wrong captions.
